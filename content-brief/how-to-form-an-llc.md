#### FAQs (for FAQ Schema)

> Sources: PAA results, competitor FAQ sections, matching terms

**Q1:** Can I form an LLC by myself without a lawyer?
**A1:** Yes. Most states let you file articles of organization online without an attorney. The process takes 15-30 minutes and costs between $40 and $500 depending on your state. A lawyer is only necessary if you have complex ownership structures, operate in multiple states, or need a customized operating agreement. For a straightforward single-member or two-member LLC, DIY filing is standard practice.
**Source:** Competitor FAQs (Wolters Kluwer), Reddit PAA

**Q2:** How long does it take to form an LLC?
**A2:** Standard processing takes 7-10 business days in most states after you submit your articles of organization. Some states (like Texas) process online filings in 2-3 days. Many states offer expedited processing for an additional fee -- typically $50-$100 -- which cuts the timeline to 1-2 business days. The EIN application through the IRS is instant if you apply online.
**Source:** Competitor FAQ (Tailor Brands)

**Q3:** Do I need a registered agent for my LLC?
**A3:** Yes -- every state requires LLCs to have a registered agent with a physical address in the state of formation. You can serve as your own registered agent (free), but your home address will become part of the public record. Commercial registered agent services cost $100-$300 per year and provide privacy plus guaranteed availability during business hours.
**Source:** Competitor FAQ (Tailor Brands)

**Q4:** What is the cheapest state to form an LLC?
**A4:** Kentucky ($40), Arkansas ($45), and Mississippi ($50) have the lowest initial filing fees. But the cheapest state to file in isn't always the best state for your business. If you form in a different state than where you operate, you'll need to register as a foreign LLC in your home state -- paying fees in both. For most small business owners, forming in your home state is the most cost-effective option overall.
**Source:** Matching terms

**Q5:** Can a single person form an LLC?
**A5:** Yes. Every state allows single-member LLCs. You get the same liability protection as a multi-member LLC. The IRS treats a single-member LLC as a "disregarded entity" by default, meaning you report business income on your personal tax return (Schedule C). You'll still want an operating agreement and a separate bank account to maintain the legal separation between you and your business.
**Source:** Competitor FAQ (Wolters Kluwer)

**Q6:** What is the difference between an LLC and a sole proprietorship?
**A6:** The main difference is liability protection. A sole proprietorship has no legal separation between you and your business -- creditors can come after your personal assets (home, savings, car) to settle business debts. An LLC creates a separate legal entity, so your personal assets are protected if the business is sued or can't pay its debts. LLCs also have more options for tax treatment and look more professional to banks and clients. The tradeoff: LLCs have formation costs and ongoing state fees, while sole proprietorships require no filing.
**Source:** Related terms, Competitor coverage
