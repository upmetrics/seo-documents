## 2. Thesis & Key Messages

### Thesis Statement

> Harvest Table Catering LLC is a full-service event catering company positioned to fill a gap in Ridgemont's growing events market — combining chef-driven menus with professional event management to serve corporate, social, and wedding clients, backed by a $175,000 startup plan and clear path to profitability by Year 2.

### Key Messages

1. The Ridgemont metro area's events market is growing but underserved by quality caterers — most options are restaurant add-ons or solo operators without scalable infrastructure.
2. Harvest Table's dual-expertise leadership (culinary + event logistics) is a genuine competitive advantage — most local caterers have one or the other, not both.
3. The per-event pricing model with three tiers (corporate, social, premium) creates revenue diversification — no single client segment accounts for more than 55% of revenue.
4. Financial projections are conservative and achievable: 85 events in Year 1 growing to 140 by Year 3, with break-even at 102 annual events.
5. The $120,000 SBA loan is well-secured by equipment assets and supported by positive EBITDA even in Year 1.

## 8. Content Outline

### Writing Tone

**For Sample Business Plan:**
- Write like a professional business consultant drafting a formal plan for a client
- Third-person throughout — "Harvest Table Catering will..." not "We will..." or "I will..."
- Every claim must be backed by a specific number, date, or named detail
- Financial sections must include the actual pre-calculated tables from Section 6B — do NOT invent new numbers
- Replace vague language with specifics: "39% year-over-year revenue growth" not "significant growth"
- Use catering industry terminology: "per-guest pricing," "BEO (banquet event order)," "event-day contract staff," "chafing dishes"
- The plan should read as if it could be submitted to Ridgemont Community Bank for the SBA 7(a) loan
- Maintain consistent use of "Harvest Table Catering," "Marcus Webb," "Priya Sharma," and "Ridgemont, Texas" throughout

**General rules:**
- **Target reading level:** Grade 8-9 (Flesch-Kincaid)
- Short paragraphs (3-4 sentences max)
- Include real numbers and specifics — not filler
- NO AI buzzwords — see BANNED AI TERMS list in the Appendix
- For the .docx version, refer to the full .md brief or ask your editor for the banned terms list

> **4 FAQs.** These address common reader questions not covered in the main plan chapters. Suitable for a blog-style sample plan page.

**Q1:** How much does it cost to start a catering business?
**A1:** Explain that startup costs vary widely ($10,000-$200,000) depending on whether the caterer operates from home, leases a commercial kitchen, or builds out a facility. Reference Harvest Table's $175,000 as a mid-range example for a fully equipped commercial operation. Mention that home-based caterers in states with cottage food laws can start for under $15,000.
**Source:** Editorial judgment — most common question from aspiring caterers
**Why not in main content:** The plan covers Harvest Table's specific costs, not general industry ranges

**Q2:** Do I need a commercial kitchen to start a catering business?
**A2:** Depends on state and local regulations. Most states require a licensed commercial kitchen for catering businesses that serve the public. Some states allow home kitchen operations under cottage food laws, but usually with revenue caps ($50,000-$75,000/year). Recommend checking with the local health department.
**Source:** Paytronix reference article mentions legal requirements; Toast mentions kitchen logistics
**Why not in main content:** Harvest Table's plan assumes a commercial kitchen — the regulatory question is tangential but commonly asked

**Q3:** How do I price my catering services?
**A3:** Cover three common pricing models: per-person (most common for catering), per-event flat rate, and cost-plus (food cost × markup multiplier). Mention that per-person pricing typically ranges $15-$150+/guest depending on service level. Reference Harvest Table's three-tier model as a practical example.
**Source:** Paytronix reference article covers three pricing models well
**Why not in main content:** The plan shows Harvest Table's specific pricing but doesn't teach pricing strategy generally

**Q4:** What licenses do I need to start a catering business?
**A4:** List the typical requirements: business license, food handler's permit/ServSafe certification, health department inspection, food service establishment permit, general liability insurance, commercial auto insurance (if delivering), and potentially a liquor license if serving alcohol. Note that requirements vary by state and city.
**Source:** Paytronix reference article's legal compliance section
**Why not in main content:** The plan lists Harvest Table's specific Texas licenses — this FAQ addresses the general question for readers in other states
