## 2. Thesis & Key Messages

### Thesis Statement

> Getting angel funding isn't about luck or knowing the right people — it's a structured, repeatable process that starts with preparation (business plan, pitch materials, research) long before you ever sit across from an investor.

### Key Messages

1. Your business plan is the first credibility test — angels fund founders who've done the homework, not just founders with exciting ideas
2. Research your target angels before reaching out — investment thesis alignment matters more than check size
3. Warm introductions are the dominant channel — cold outreach converts below 5%, so invest in your network first
4. Your pitch materials (deck, one-pager, elevator pitch) must tell a cohesive, concise story across formats
5. Negotiation doesn't have to be adversarial — SAFEs and standardized terms make early-stage deals straightforward

#### H2: Start With a Solid Business Plan
> ~250 words, 3-4 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: The business plan isn't paperwork — it's your first filter. If you can't articulate market size, revenue model, and competitive advantage on paper, you're not ready to pitch. Most founders skip this and it shows within 30 seconds of an investor meeting.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "Before you Google 'angel investors near me,' stop. You need to answer one question first: can you explain how your business makes money — clearly, specifically, and without buzzwords?"
> 🔗 Transition from previous: Bridge from intro's thesis ("preparation game") — "And that preparation starts with the document most founders rush past."
> 🚫 Avoid: Don't turn this into a "how to write a business plan" guide — that's a separate article. Stay focused on WHY a business plan matters for angel fundraising and WHAT angels specifically look for in it.
> 📖 Reference: SeedBlink's section on "What do angel investors look for at pre-seed?" — good list of investor expectations. Improve by connecting each expectation back to business plan sections.

Write about what angels specifically evaluate in a business plan: market opportunity (is the TAM real?), revenue model clarity, team credibility, and financial projections that aren't fantasy. Emphasize that a business plan forces founders to confront hard questions BEFORE an investor does — and that's the point.

Mention that angel investors are typically accredited investors ($200K+ income or $1M+ net worth per SEC definition) — they're sophisticated evaluators, not friends-and-family handing over a check. Your plan needs to reflect that seriousness.

End with a brief mention that tools like Upmetrics make building an investor-ready business plan faster — link naturally, not as a pitch.

> 🖼️ Visual: None — this section is argumentative, not data-heavy.
> 💡 ProTip: "Your financial projections don't need to be perfect — but they need to be defensible. If an investor asks 'why do you expect 40% margins?' you should have a real answer, not a shrug."

#### H2: Build Your Pitch Materials
> ~300 words, 4-5 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: You need three formats of the same story: a pitch deck (10-15 slides), a one-pager (leave-behind summary), and a 60-second elevator pitch. These aren't three separate projects — they're one narrative at different zoom levels. Most founders only build the deck and struggle when asked for the other two.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "A pitch deck alone isn't enough. You need the same story in three formats — and you need to be able to switch between them on demand."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "With a solid plan behind you, the next step is turning it into pitch-ready materials that match how investors actually evaluate deals."
> 🚫 Avoid: Don't list 15 slides and what goes on each one — that's what the linked pitch deck articles cover. Focus on the STRATEGY: why three formats, what goes in each, and how they connect.
> 📖 Reference: DigitalOcean's "Before you meet investors" section covers pitch deck basics — borrow the checklist concept but keep it tighter and add the one-pager and elevator pitch dimensions they miss.

Cover the three formats:
1. **Pitch deck** (10-15 slides): Problem, solution, market, traction, team, financials, ask. Link to Upmetrics pitch deck resources.
2. **One-pager**: A single-page summary investors can forward to others. Include: business name, problem/solution, traction, team, funding ask, contact. This is the most underrated fundraising document.
3. **Elevator pitch** (60 seconds): The verbal version — who you are, what you do, why now, what you need. Practice until it sounds natural, not rehearsed.

Emphasize consistency: if your deck says $500K raise and your one-pager says $750K, you look disorganized.

> 🖼️ Visual: Inline table comparing the three pitch formats — columns: Format, Length, When You Use It, Key Content. Alt text: "Comparison table showing pitch deck, one-pager, and elevator pitch formats with length and usage context."
> 💡 ProTip: "Send your one-pager first, not your deck. It's a lower-commitment ask and a better conversation starter. If the investor is interested, they'll ask for the deck."

#### H2: Find and Research the Right Angels
> ~300 words, 4-5 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: "Finding" angels isn't hard — AngelList alone has thousands. The real work is QUALIFYING them. You want investors whose thesis, industry focus, and check size align with your round. Pitching a healthcare-focused angel on your e-commerce startup wastes both your time.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "The goal isn't to find ANY angel investor. It's to find the RIGHT three to five who already invest in your space."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "Now that your materials are ready, the question is: who do you send them to?"
> 🚫 Avoid: Don't make this a list of every angel platform that exists — keep it to 3-4 with specific advice for each. Don't suggest spray-and-pray outreach.
> 📖 Reference: DigitalOcean's detailed platform breakdown (AngelList, Gust, Angel Capital Association, Tech Coast Angels) is the best reference — borrow the platform list but add the research/qualification layer they miss entirely.

Cover two phases:

**Phase 1 — Discovery (where to look):**
- Angel networks and groups (Angel Capital Association directory, local angel groups)
- Online platforms (AngelList, Gust)
- Pitch competitions and demo days
- Accelerator alumni networks
- Brief context on where angel rounds fit in the broader funding stages (link to funding rounds article)

**Phase 2 — Research (qualifying each angel):**
- Check their portfolio — do they invest in your industry/stage?
- Read their blog posts, tweets, or podcast interviews — what's their thesis?
- Look at check size — typical angel range is $25K-$100K per deal
- Note any portfolio companies that overlap or conflict with yours

> 🖼️ Visual: None needed — the two-phase structure keeps this section organized without a graphic.
> 💡 ProTip: "Before you email a single investor, build a spreadsheet: name, fund/group, thesis, check size, portfolio companies, and mutual connections. Twenty targeted pitches beat two hundred cold ones."

#### H2: Get Warm Introductions (Not Cold Emails)
> ~250 words, 3-4 paragraphs | 🟡 SHOULD HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: Cold outreach to angel investors converts below 5%. Warm introductions through mutual connections are how the majority of angel deals actually begin. This isn't unfair — it's how trust works. The good news: you probably have more connections than you think.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "Cold emails to investors have a response rate under 5%. Before you write one, ask yourself: do I know anyone who knows this person?"
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "You've identified your target investors. Now you need a way in — and that almost always means a warm introduction."
> 🚫 Avoid: Don't make this feel discouraging for founders without big networks. Acknowledge the challenge, then provide practical paths (accelerators, co-founders' networks, other founders, LinkedIn mutual connections, angel group open events).
> 📖 Reference: VLP Law Group's "Building Long-Term Relationships" section — borrow the relationship-first framing but make it specific: WHO do you ask for intros, HOW do you ask, and what's the format of a good intro request.

Cover the mechanics:
- **Who to ask:** Co-founders' former colleagues, advisors, other founders who've raised, accelerator mentors, lawyers/accountants who work with startups
- **How to ask:** Make it easy for the introducer — write a forwardable blurb (2-3 sentences: who you are, what you're building, why this specific investor). Don't ask someone to "put in a good word" — give them the exact words.
- **Plan B — warm-adjacent channels:** Angel group open pitch nights, demo days, and startup events where investors are explicitly attending to hear pitches. These aren't cold — you're invited.

> 🖼️ Visual: None — this section is narrative and short enough to not need a visual break.
> 💡 ProTip: None.

#### H2: Pitch, Negotiate, and Close
> ~250 words, 3-4 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: The pitch meeting itself gets outsized attention, but the negotiation and close are where deals actually happen or die. Most angel deals today use SAFEs (Simple Agreements for Future Equity) — standardized documents that don't require expensive lawyers. The negotiation is simpler than founders expect.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "You got the meeting. Now what? The pitch is actually the easy part — what comes next determines whether you get a check."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "A warm intro gets you in the room. Here's how to make the meeting count — and close the deal after."
> 🚫 Avoid: Don't write a full pitch coaching section — focus on the mechanics of what happens AFTER the pitch: follow-up, terms, and closing. Don't make negotiation sound adversarial.
> 📖 Reference: SeedBlink's equity expectation ranges (5-15% pre-seed, 15-20% seed) — borrow these specific numbers. Neither DigitalOcean nor VLP covers deal terms well.

Cover three beats:
1. **The pitch itself:** Keep it to 15-20 minutes. Leave time for questions — angels learn more from your answers than your slides. Know your numbers cold.
2. **Terms and structure:** Most angel deals use post-money SAFEs (Y Combinator's free template is the standard). Typical equity: 10-15% for angel rounds. Explain what a valuation cap is in one sentence. Link to fair equity percentage article.
3. **Closing:** Follow up within 24 hours. Be ready to share your data room (business plan, cap table, financials). Remember that due diligence goes both ways — link to questions to ask investors.

> 🖼️ Visual: None — section is concise enough without one.
> 💡 ProTip: "Don't negotiate against yourself. State your terms clearly, back them with data, and let the investor respond. Silence after stating your ask is fine — let it sit."

## 9. FAQ Section

> 4 FAQs — each addresses a common follow-up question NOT covered in the main outline.

**Q1:** How much equity should you give an angel investor?
**A1:** Typical angel rounds give up 10-15% equity at the pre-seed stage and 15-20% at seed. The exact percentage depends on your valuation, the amount raised, and how many investors are in the round. Use a SAFE with a valuation cap to set a ceiling.
**Source:** SeedBlink reference article + editorial judgment
**Why not in main content:** The outline's "Pitch, Negotiate, and Close" section mentions equity ranges briefly but doesn't go deep — a FAQ can give a specific number readers are searching for.

**Q2:** How long does it take to raise an angel round?
**A2:** Plan for 3-6 months from first outreach to close. Some rounds close in weeks (if you have strong traction and warm intros), but most first-time founders underestimate the timeline. Start fundraising 6+ months before you need the money.
**Source:** Editorial judgment — none of the reference articles covered timelines
**Why not in main content:** Timeline guidance is useful but tangential to the step-by-step process the article covers.

**Q3:** What's the difference between angel investors and venture capitalists?
**A3:** Angels invest their personal money, typically $25K-$100K per deal, at the earliest stages. VCs manage institutional funds, invest larger amounts ($500K-$10M+), and usually enter at seed or Series A. Angels are often more flexible on terms and faster to decide.
**Source:** DigitalOcean and SeedBlink reference articles
**Why not in main content:** The article assumes readers have already decided on angel funding — re-explaining VC vs. angel would slow the narrative.

**Q4:** Can you raise angel funding without a working product?
**A4:** Yes, but you need strong compensating signals: a clear prototype or mockup, early waitlist signups, LOIs from potential customers, or deep domain expertise. Angels invest in the team and opportunity at this stage — but "just an idea" with nothing to show rarely closes a round anymore.
**Source:** Editorial judgment + DigitalOcean mention of traction signals
**Why not in main content:** The outline focuses on what to DO, not on minimum requirements — this FAQ addresses a common gating question.

unlock, unleash, harness, leverage (verb), elevate, delve, dive into, deep dive, navigate (metaphorical), landscape, realm, tapestry, beacon, game-changer, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, next-level, seamless(ly), robust, holistic, empower, supercharge, revolutionize, transform (hyperbolic), journey (metaphorical), bustling, thriving, vibrant, comprehensive guide, ultimate guide, definitive guide (in body copy), moreover, furthermore, additionally, in essence, foster, facilitate, streamline (filler), myriad, plethora, multifaceted, pivotal, paramount, indispensable, crucial/essential (empty emphasis), firstly/secondly/lastly, ensuring, utilizing, noteworthy, remarkably, undoubtedly, rest assured, with that said, key takeaways, actionable insights, in today's [X] world, it's important to note, it's worth mentioning, when it comes to, in the world of, look no further, whether you're a...or a..., not just...but also, it goes without saying, take [X] to the next level, tailored to your needs, in conclusion, to sum up

**Use instead:** plain verbs, specific nouns, real numbers, direct statements, conversational phrasing.
