### 2B. Intent Mismatch Analysis

**The target page has a SIGNIFICANT intent mismatch.** It is structured as an educational "how-to" guide — it teaches readers how to create a financial plan in 7 steps. But the SERP clearly rewards pages that provide actual examples and downloadable templates.

**Specific mismatches:**
1. **Title says "w/ example" but the example is buried.** The financial plan example section exists (H2: "Startup financial plan example") but it's deep in the article. Searchers scanning quickly may not find it.
2. **No downloadable template within the content.** The page links to Upmetrics sample plans in CTA sidebar blocks, but has no embedded downloadable Word/Excel/PDF financial plan template that a reader can grab immediately. Every top-ranking competitor either IS a downloadable template or prominently offers one.
3. **"How to Prepare" framing vs. "Example" expectation.** The title promises preparation guidance, but the keyword intent leans toward "show me an example I can reference." The page tries to serve both intents but doesn't fully satisfy either.

**Why a DR39 PDF (position 7) outranks DR90 pages:** The marshalltown.org PDF is literally a startup business plan template. It perfectly matches the "example" intent — no reading required, just download and fill in. Google is telling us: for this query, a mediocre template beats an excellent guide.

### 2C. Content-Reader Alignment Check

**Does the page answer the primary need within the first scroll?** Partially. The page opens with "What is startup financial planning?" — a definitional intro that doesn't match the searcher's urgency. A searcher looking for "financial plan for startup business example" already knows what a financial plan is; they want to see one.

**Does it deliver what the title promises?** The title says "(w/ example)" and the page does contain an example section, but it's positioned as the 4th H2 — after 2,000+ words of educational content. The example should be surfaced much earlier or at least prominently linked from the intro.

**Is there a clear value proposition?** Not immediately. The page doesn't differentiate itself from competitors within the first 200 words. It doesn't say "Here's a complete startup financial plan example you can download" or "We'll walk you through a real coffee shop financial plan with numbers."

**Recommendation:** Restructure the page to lead with the example/template value proposition and make the educational content secondary. → See Section 5C for detailed content recommendations.

### 4C. Content Flow & Structure Issues

1. **Intro is too long (300+ words before first H2).** The personal anecdote ("I've always wondered why most entrepreneurs hate financial projections") is engaging but delays getting to the value. For a searcher who typed "financial plan for startup business example," this intro doesn't deliver the "example" they're looking for.

2. **H2 "What is startup financial planning?" delays the core content.** This definitional section is ~400 words that most searchers (who already know what a financial plan is) will skip. It should be shortened to 1-2 sentences or moved to a sidebar/callout.

3. **Steps section (H2: "How to create...") is the strongest content** but is preceded by ~800 words of setup. The step-by-step guide should start within the first 200-300 words.

4. **H2: "Key components of your startup financial plan" overlaps with Step 5.** Step 5 says "There are really just four core statements" and then this H2 repeats the same four statements with more detail. This is structural duplication that could be consolidated.

5. **H2: "Tools and resources for financial planning" is very thin.** It's essentially a CTA for Upmetrics without genuine comparison or resource recommendations.

6. **H2: "Plan your financials using Upmetrics" is a pure promotional section.** Combined with the 10 CTA blocks, this makes the page feel sales-heavy.

7. **FAQ section is well-structured** with 7+ questions and FAQPage schema — this is a strength.

### 4D. Readability Issues

1. **CTA blocks interrupt reading flow.** With 10 CTA blocks in a 3,998-word article, there's roughly one CTA every 400 words. Several appear to be full-width promotional blocks mid-content (e.g., "Say goodbye to old-school excel sheets & templates" between steps).

2. **The financial plan example section (balance sheet data)** presents raw numbers in a table format without sufficient context. Readers see negative cash balances (-$169,704.82) without explanation of why these are negative — this could confuse beginners.

3. **Passive voice clusters:** "Your startup costs can be estimated..." / "these costs are categorized into..." — several sections use passive constructions when active voice would be clearer and more engaging.
