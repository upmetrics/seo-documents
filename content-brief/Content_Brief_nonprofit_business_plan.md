##### H3: 1. Executive Summary
> ~200 words, 3-4 paragraphs
> Explain that this is a 1-2 page overview of the entire plan — written LAST but placed first. Include: mission statement, key programs, funding needs, and growth goals. Give a sentence starter: "Our organization, [Name], is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit that..."
> Competitor reference: Boardsource and SD Legal both cover this — but neither gives writing tips. SD Legal notes it should be written last, which is good advice to include.
> 📈 Stats to find: None.
> 🚫 Avoid: Don't write a full sample executive summary — just give the structure and a starter sentence.

##### H3: 2. Mission and Vision Statements
> ~150 words, 2-3 paragraphs
> Explain the difference between mission (what you do today) and vision (what the world looks like if you succeed). Give examples of strong vs weak mission statements. Mention that the mission statement is the foundation everything else builds on.
> Competitor reference: US Chamber and SD Legal both cover this. US Chamber's is best — reference their approach but add examples.
> 📈 Stats to find: None.

##### H3: 3. Organization Overview
> ~150 words, 2-3 paragraphs
> Cover: legal structure (501(c)(3) status), founding date, location, history, and current stage. Mention that new organizations should describe what they plan to build. Include organizational values if relevant.
> Competitor reference: Boardsource covers this well — note the founder story angle.
> 📈 Stats to find: None.

##### H3: 4. Programs and Services
> ~200 words, 3-4 paragraphs
> Describe each program the nonprofit runs or plans to run. For each: who it serves, how it works, what outcomes it produces. Advise writers to be specific — "after-school tutoring for 200 low-income students in grades 3-8" beats "educational programs for youth."
> Competitor reference: Boardsource and US Chamber both cover this. US Chamber ties it back to mission alignment — use that angle.
> 📈 Stats to find: None.
> 🚫 Avoid: Don't let this section become a laundry list. Focus on 2-3 core programs.

##### H3: 5. Market and Needs Analysis
> ~200 words, 3-4 paragraphs
> Explain that "market analysis" for nonprofits means "needs assessment" — who needs your services and how you know. Include: target population demographics, geographic focus, data proving the need exists, and what other organizations are doing (or not doing) to address it.
> Competitor reference: Boardsource frames this as "industry analysis" and "customer analysis" (two separate sections). SD Legal calls it "market analysis and needs assessment." Combine both approaches.
> 📈 Stats to find: Suggest the writer find local census data, community health assessments, or similar needs data relevant to their nonprofit's focus area.
> 🚫 Avoid: Don't use corporate market analysis jargon like "TAM" or "market share" — frame everything in terms of community need.

##### H3: 6. Marketing and Outreach Strategy
> ~150 words, 2-3 paragraphs
> Cover how the nonprofit will raise awareness: social media, community events, partnerships, media outreach, email campaigns. Frame it as "outreach" not just "marketing" — nonprofits need to reach both beneficiaries AND donors.
> Competitor reference: Boardsource and SD Legal both cover this briefly. Neither mentions the dual audience (beneficiaries + donors).
> 📈 Stats to find: None.

##### H3: 7. Fundraising Plan
> ~200 words, 3-4 paragraphs
> This is the nonprofit equivalent of a revenue model. Cover funding sources: individual donations, grants (government and foundation), corporate sponsorships, fundraising events, membership fees, earned income (if any). Advise diversifying — relying on one source is risky.

#### FAQs (for FAQ Schema)

> **6 FAQs included.** 4 from PAA, 2 from Ahrefs keyword data / competitor gaps.
> **Deduplication rule:** Every FAQ below has been checked against the outline above — none of these questions are answered in the main content sections.

**Q1:** What is the 80/20 rule for nonprofits?
**A1:** Explain that the 80/20 rule in nonprofit fundraising means that roughly 80% of donations come from 20% of donors. This is why major donor cultivation and retention is so important for nonprofit sustainability. Mention that this principle should inform the fundraising plan section of the business plan.
**Source:** PAA
**Why not in main content:** Specific fundraising principle — tangential to the business plan writing process

**Q2:** Can I pay myself if I run a nonprofit?
**A2:** Yes — nonprofit founders and employees can receive reasonable compensation. The key word is "reasonable" — the IRS scrutinizes nonprofit salaries that are significantly above market rate. Explain that compensation should be documented in the operations section of the business plan and approved by the board.
**Source:** PAA
**Why not in main content:** Compensation policy is tangential to business plan structure — commonly asked by first-time founders

**Q3:** Which is better, LLC or nonprofit?
**A3:** Explain that it depends on the goal. An LLC is better for businesses that want to generate profit for owners. A nonprofit (501(c)(3)) is better for organizations focused on a charitable mission that qualifies for tax-exempt status and can receive tax-deductible donations. Briefly note that some states allow nonprofit LLCs, but most nonprofits incorporate as nonprofit corporations.
**Source:** PAA
**Why not in main content:** Legal structure choice — happens before the business plan writing process

**Q4:** How long should a nonprofit business plan be?
**A4:** Most nonprofit business plans are 15-25 pages. A startup nonprofit's plan might be closer to 10-15 pages, while an established organization with multiple programs could go up to 30. The key is covering all essential sections without padding. Grant reviewers prefer concise plans over lengthy ones.
**Source:** Ahrefs keyword: "how long does it take to make a business plan" (volume: 50)
**Why not in main content:** Length guidance is mentioned briefly in the tips section but deserves a direct answer for FAQ schema

**Q5:** Do nonprofits need a business plan?
**A5:** Yes. A business plan helps nonprofits secure grants, attract board members, plan finances, and stay focused on their mission. Many grant applications specifically require a business plan or strategic plan. Even small nonprofits benefit from documenting their programs, funding strategy, and financial projections.
**Source:** Asana FAQ schema + Ahrefs keyword match
**Why not in main content:** The "why" section covers reasons in depth — this FAQ gives a direct yes/no answer for the snippet

**Q6:** What are the 3 C's of a business plan?
**A6:** The 3 C's of a business plan are Company, Customers, and Competitors. For nonprofits, think of these as: your Organization (mission, team, programs), your Community (the people you serve), and your Landscape (other nonprofits addressing similar needs). This framework can help simplify the planning process.
**Source:** PAA
**Why not in main content:** General business plan framework — tangential to nonprofit-specific planning
