## 2. Thesis & Key Messages

### Thesis Statement

> Marigold Lane Soap Co., LLC seeks an $85,000 SBA 7(a) microloan to launch a cold-process handmade-soap manufacturer in Ashford Falls, Vermont, projecting $185,000 in Year 1 revenue, $18,648 in Year 1 net income, and a 31% margin of safety above break-even.

### Key Messages

1. The business is built on a four-channel revenue model (DTC, wholesale, farmers' market, custom B&B) that captures different price points and de-risks any single channel.
2. Cold-process soap has a four-to-six-week cure cycle, which dictates production capacity and inventory days — the operations plan reflects this reality, not a generic manufacturing schedule.
3. Year 1 financials assume 22% materials cost, 10% direct labor, 67.1% gross margin, and a $42,000 owner draw — conservative, defensible, and aligned with industry benchmarks for craft soap manufacturers.
4. FDA and Vermont state regulatory compliance is a real cost line and a real risk. The plan addresses true-soap vs. cosmetic classification, labeling rules, and product-liability insurance head-on.
5. The Year 1 break-even is 19,082 units / $141,207 — well below the Year 1 forecast of 25,000 units / $185,000, giving Marigold Lane a 31% margin of safety in its launch year.

### What to Borrow / What to Avoid

**Borrow:**
- Reference 2's chapter structure for non-financial sections — pulling Pricing, Sales Channels, and Customer Retention out as their own chapters reads like a real plan, not a high-school template.
- Reference 3's KPI emphasis on contribution margin, breakeven units, and unit volume — soap businesses live or die on these metrics. Anchor the Financial Plan around them.
- Reference 1's market-segmentation logic: name each segment, explain why they buy, and quantify if possible. Marigold Lane's segments are different (DTC online, wholesale retailers, farmers' market shoppers, B&B operators) but the logic holds.
- All three include a named or implied founder with a credibility-building backstory — Marigold Lane's plan should center Sarah Keller's chemistry and side-business track record the same way.

**Avoid:**
- Placeholder financial tables (every reference fails here). Marigold Lane's financials are pre-calculated and math-validated in Section 6B — use them verbatim.
- Vague competitive analysis ("competitive product prices based on market averages"). Name three specific Vermont/New England craft soap competitors plus one or two national reference points and lay out a real comparison table.
- Unsourced market statistics. Every market number must cite a source from the External Links table in Section 6.
- Skipping FDA and Vermont labeling regulations. This is the differentiator — competitors all skip it; we go deep enough that a real soap maker would learn something.
- Aggressive financial assumptions without justification (Reference 3's "2-month breakeven"). Year 1 break-even comes in month 9–10 for Marigold Lane based on the math — say so plainly.
