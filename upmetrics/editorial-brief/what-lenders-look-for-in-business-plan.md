## 2. Thesis & Key Messages

### Thesis Statement

> Lenders and investors read the same business plan through different lenses — lenders scan it for repayment risk, investors scan it for return potential — and a plan that wins funding answers both audiences in the same document, section by section.

### Key Messages

1. Both lenders and investors use your business plan to filter risk before they ever meet you — most "no" decisions are made on the plan alone.
2. Each plan section has a specific job to do for the reader. If it does not serve that job, the reader assumes the business has weak fundamentals.
3. Historical financials and debt service coverage are where most loan applications quietly get killed — more than any other section.
4. Projections need to show two stories: what happens without funding, and what changes with it. Otherwise the reader cannot judge why the money matters.
5. The plan does not stand alone — personal credit, collateral, character, and supporting docs finish the picture the plan starts.

### The Outline

#### H1: What Lenders (and Investors) Really Look For in a Business Plan

##### Introduction
> ~180 words | 🔴 MUST HAVE
>
> Hook: Open with the reality check — when a lender picks up your business plan, they are not reading it the way you wrote it. They are scanning for answers to a small number of very specific questions, and skipping everything that does not help them answer those questions.
> Context: Most founders write their plan for themselves, or for a generic "reader." Lenders and investors are not generic. They have a decision to make, and a framework for making it.
> Promise: This article walks through every part of a business plan a lender or investor actually reads, what they are looking for in each, and how to present it so the answer is clear on first pass.
> Thesis preview: Lenders and investors read the same plan for different reasons — but both have concrete expectations for every section, and plans that meet those expectations get funded.
> Source inspiration: Nav's "this is how your file is read" framing works well — adopt that posture from sentence one.

---

#### H2: Business History and Operating Track Record
> ~200 words, 2–3 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: History is not nostalgia — lenders read it to gauge whether you have handled adversity before. Frame it as proof of operating maturity, not storytelling.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "The 'About Us' section of your plan is the first thing a lender reads — and it is the first place they decide whether you are a seasoned operator or a first-timer."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: After the intro sets up the lender-as-reader frame, open this section by saying the first thing a lender actually looks for is whether your business has a track record at all — and if it does, what that track record tells them.
> 🚫 Avoid: Do not turn this into an origin story. Skip the "it all started in a garage" cliché. No personal anecdotes unless they demonstrate operating judgment.
> 📖 Reference: None specific — lean on editorial judgment.

Writing instruction: Cover what to include — founding date, original vision, major pivots, how revenue has grown, specific challenges faced and how the business responded (supply-chain hit, customer concentration event, team turnover, etc.). Emphasize that lenders read this section for evidence of operating maturity. For pre-revenue startups, explain what to write instead: relevant operator experience of the founding team, results from prior ventures, or results from a pilot.

Add an **Investor view** callout: investors read this for signal on founder grit and pattern recognition — they weigh prior startup experience, failures survived, and evidence of market insight.

> 🖼️ Visual: None required for this section.
> 💡 ProTip: Pro tip — if you cannot name at least one concrete challenge you have already handled, the section is incomplete. Handling adversity is the whole point of this section for a lender.

---

#### H2: Revenue Model — How the Business Actually Makes Money
> ~200 words, 2–3 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: Lenders want to see the mechanics of cash generation, not marketing copy about "value." Keep it operational.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "Every dollar a lender gives you has to come back. This section tells them how."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "Once a lender understands where you have been, they want to understand how you actually earn a dollar today."
> 🚫 Avoid: Do not describe your "value proposition" as the revenue model. Customers pay for a thing, in a way, on a schedule — that is the revenue model.
> 📖 Reference: None specific.

Writing instruction: Walk through what to specify — product or service description, pricing structure (one-time, subscription, retainer, unit-based, per-event), channel (direct, retail, wholesale, online), sales cycle length, payment terms (Net 30, prepaid, card-on-file), and how receivables are actually collected. Show how these mechanics produce predictable or lumpy cash flow — lenders care about the difference.

Add an **Investor view** callout: investors also want margin structure, LTV/CAC dynamics (for SaaS/consumer), and what drives pricing power. Lender cares if the dollars arrive. Investor cares how much of each dollar stays.

> 🖼️ Visual: Optional — simple inline table showing revenue streams with columns: Stream / Pricing / Payment Terms / % of Revenue. Alt text: "Revenue model summary table."
> 💡 ProTip: Pro tip — include a one-line statement of typical payment terms ("We invoice Net 30; average collection is 38 days"). It signals working-capital awareness, which lenders notice.

---

#### H2: The Management Team Behind the Plan
> ~200 words, 2–3 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: Relevant experience beats impressive experience. Our take is that lenders want domain fit, not resume padding.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "Lenders fund people, not plans. This is the section where they decide whether to trust yours."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "Knowing how the business makes money is only useful if the team behind it can execute. That is what this section proves."
> 🚫 Avoid: Generic bios. Do not list every job every team member has ever held. Do not pad with advisors unless they are operationally involved.
> 📖 Reference: None specific.

Writing instruction: Explain what to include — full names, roles, years of relevant industry experience, specific wins tied to this business's success pattern (built a unit of similar size, managed a P&L of this scale, ran this kind of operation through a downturn). For each key person, one to two sentences tying their experience to what the business needs to do in the next 36 months. Call out gaps honestly — if the team is missing a CFO or ops lead, say so and name the plan to fill it.

Add an **Investor view** callout: investors weigh founder-market fit, prior exits, and complementarity of the founding team more than lenders do. Lenders default to the primary guarantor.

> 🖼️ Visual: None required.
> 💡 ProTip: Pro tip — if your team has real gaps, naming them pre-emptively builds more credibility than hiding them. Lenders will find the gap anyway.

---

#### H2: Market Opportunity and Competitive Positioning
> ~220 words, 3–4 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: Lenders need to believe the market exists and is big enough to support the debt. Investors need to believe it can grow. Both need proof — not assertions.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "Every lender has seen a dozen plans claim a multi-billion-dollar market. They have stopped believing the claim — they read this section for proof instead."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "Even the strongest team cannot rescue a business in a market that is not there. That is what this section establishes."
> 🚫 Avoid: Do not cite an industry TAM number without a reputable source. Do not use the "$X billion market" framing without narrowing to your *addressable* slice.
> 📖 Reference: Use [Upmetrics' market research guide](https://upmetrics.co/blog/types-of-market-research) as a supporting link — do not summarize it.

Writing instruction: Cover what a lender looks for: who your customers are, how many there are in your trade area or addressable segment, whether the segment is growing or shrinking, and how you differ from the 3–5 most relevant competitors. Include current marketing and acquisition motions — regular social activity, partnerships, referrals, paid channels — to show ongoing demand generation, not just intent.

Add an **Investor view** callout: investors want TAM/SAM/SOM sizing, tailwinds (regulatory, demographic, technology), and defensibility (network effect, brand, proprietary tech). Lenders want to confirm you will still have customers in year three.

> 🖼️ Visual: Strongly suggested — inline competitor comparison table with columns: Competitor / Strengths / Weaknesses / How we differ. Alt text: "Competitor comparison table."
> 💡 ProTip: Pro tip — cite trade association data or a government source (Census, BLS, IBISWorld summary) rather than unsourced round numbers. It costs nothing and instantly raises credibility.

---

#### H2: Historical Financials and Debt Service Coverage
> ~260 words, 3–4 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: This is the section that decides most loan files. If your debt service coverage ratio is wrong or missing, nothing else matters.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "More small business loans die in this section than in all the others combined."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "Once a lender believes the market will pay you, they open the financials to see whether the math actually works."
> 🚫 Avoid: Do not paste exported software reports without cleanup. Do not round away meaningful detail. Do not omit the DSCR calculation — include it explicitly.
> 📖 Reference: Alloy Dev Co's underwriting walkthrough is useful on DSCR math — borrow the step-by-step format.

Writing instruction: Cover what to include — 2 to 3 years of income statements, balance sheets, and cash-flow statements; year-to-date interim statements if the fiscal year is in progress; and the explicit debt service coverage ratio calculation. DSCR = (Net Operating Income + Depreciation + Interest) ÷ Total Annual Debt Service. Most SBA and conventional lenders want DSCR of at least 1.25x; some want 1.5x. Show the math — both historical (looking back) and pro-forma (with the new debt included). Explain how add-backs work (owner salary, one-time expenses) so the writer's readers can surface them correctly.

Link to [common reasons SBA loans get denied](https://upmetrics.co/blog/sba-loan-denied) immediately after naming cash-flow coverage as the top rejection reason.

Add an **Investor view** callout: investors do not care about DSCR — they care about unit economics, gross margin trajectory, and burn. Same data, different question: "Does the money compound?" not "Will the debt be paid back?"

> 🖼️ Visual: Recommended — inline HTML table showing a worked DSCR example. Columns: Line Item / Year 1 / Year 2 / Year 3. Rows: NOI, Depreciation, Interest, Total Debt Service, **DSCR**. Alt text: "Worked DSCR calculation example."
> 💡 ProTip: Pro tip — if your historical DSCR is below 1.25x, address it directly in the plan. Explain the one-time factor that drove it down, and show the pro-forma number that brings it above threshold. Silence here is a rejection trigger.

#### H2: Forward Projections — With and Without Funding
> ~220 words, 3–4 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: The projection is not a forecast — it is an argument for why the money matters. You need two versions.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "If your projections look the same with or without the loan, why does the lender need to fund you?"
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "Historicals tell the lender where you have been. Projections tell them what is coming — and what the money changes."
> 🚫 Avoid: Hockey-stick charts. Single-scenario forecasts. Numbers without stated assumptions.
> 📖 Reference: Use [Upmetrics' newsletter on worst-case forecasting](https://upmetrics.co/newsletter/investors-fund-the-worst-case-not-the-best) as a supporting link after the paragraph on why hockey-stick forecasts backfire.

Writing instruction: Cover a 3-year projection of income statement, cash flow, and balance sheet. Include two scenarios explicitly: with funding (what the loan or investment enables) and without it (what the business looks like if declined). Quantify what the money changes — new equipment throughput, headcount hired, market expanded. Include seasonality or cyclicality if the industry has it (restaurant, retail, agriculture, construction). List every assumption on a separate line so the lender can press on it.

Add an **Investor view** callout: investors read projections for the shape of the curve — steepness, leverage points, and cash-burn window. Lenders read projections for the minimum DSCR across the forecast period. Give both.

> 🖼️ Visual: Recommended — side-by-side comparison graphic: "With funding vs. Without funding" showing Revenue, Net Income, and Cash. Alt text: "Projections comparison: with funding vs. without funding."
> 💡 ProTip: Pro tip — model a 20% revenue miss as a third scenario. Plans that show a downside case and still cover debt service stand out in the file.

---

#### H2: Collateral and Available Assets
> ~180 words, 2–3 paragraphs | 🟡 SHOULD HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: Collateral is the lender's escape hatch. Think like a used-equipment appraiser when you describe it.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "Lenders do not want to repossess anything. They just want to know they could."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "Projections tell the lender you can repay. Collateral tells them what happens if you cannot."
> 🚫 Avoid: Listing assets at book value only. Listing assets you do not actually own yet. Overstating value of specialized equipment.
> 📖 Reference: None specific.

Writing instruction: Cover what to include — owned equipment with approximate current market value and age, real property, inventory (with turnover cadence), vehicles, IP where applicable, and any pledged personal assets (home equity, rental properties, investment accounts). Explain how lenders discount collateral — equipment at 50–70% of forced-sale value, inventory at 20–50%, real estate at 75–80% of appraised value. SBA typically requires collateral when available but will not decline solely for shortfall above $50,000 if other factors are strong.

Link to [major equipment and asset purchases](https://upmetrics.co/blog/purchase-planning-for-small-businesses) in the paragraph that discusses asset-backed lending.

Add an **Investor view** callout: equity investors rarely ask for collateral — they take equity instead. This section is primarily lender-facing.

> 🖼️ Visual: None required.
> 💡 ProTip: Pro tip — real estate is by far the strongest collateral; specialized equipment is the weakest. A plan that leads with the strongest asset first reads better.

#### H2: The Purpose of the Loan (or Investment)
> ~180 words, 2–3 paragraphs | 🔴 MUST HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: Vague use-of-funds language kills files. Specific line-item allocation closes them.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "Lenders do not fund 'growth.' They fund a truck, a hood system, a build-out, a hire."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "Everything to this point has proved the business. This section tells the lender what you actually want the money for."
> 🚫 Avoid: "Working capital" as a line item with no breakdown. Round numbers without quotes or invoices to back them.
> 📖 Reference: None specific.

Writing instruction: Cover a line-item use-of-funds table — equipment with quoted cost, leasehold improvements with contractor estimate, initial inventory, working capital broken into payroll runway + marketing + AR float, professional fees, and contingency. Tie the dollar total back to the request, and tie each line to a revenue or operational outcome in the projection. Reference common use categories briefly — startup, expansion, acquisition, equipment purchase, refinance, working capital.

Link to [SBA loan to buy an existing business](https://upmetrics.co/blog/sba-loan-to-buy-business) in the list of common use-of-funds categories under acquisitions.

Include a brief note that if the purpose does not fit an SBA product, [non-SBA lending options](https://upmetrics.co/blog/sba-loan-alternatives) may be a better match — link naturally in the final paragraph.

Add an **Investor view** callout: investors call this "use of proceeds" and expect a time horizon (12- or 18-month runway), KPIs each dollar will move, and how the next round will be triggered.

> 🖼️ Visual: Inline use-of-funds table. Columns: Category / Amount / Why / Supporting doc. Alt text: "Use of funds breakdown table."
> 💡 ProTip: Pro tip — attach vendor quotes or a construction estimate in the appendix. It turns your use-of-funds from a claim into a fact.

#### H2: Beyond the Business Plan — What Else They Check
> ~220 words, 3–4 paragraphs | 🟡 SHOULD HAVE
> 🎯 Our angle: The plan starts the decision. It does not finish it. A few lender-side items can kill an otherwise strong file.
> 🪝 Suggested hook: "Your plan can be perfect and still get declined. Here is what else they are pulling while they read it."
> 🔗 Transition from previous: "You have covered every section of the plan. The plan is now doing its job — and the lender is running a parallel workflow you do not see."
> 🚫 Avoid: Listing every possible document. Keep it to the decision-driving items.
> 📖 Reference: Nav's underwriting explainer covers this well — borrow the list but cut to essentials.

Writing instruction: Cover what lenders review alongside the plan — personal credit (for SBA loans, the primary guarantor's FICO SBSS is a gating factor; SBA minimum moved to 165 under the 2025 SOP), business credit profile, personal financial statement (SBA Form 413), two to three years of personal and business tax returns, resumes of key principals, citizenship/residency verification for SBA programs, criminal background check (SBA Form 912), and a secondary source of repayment where applicable.

Close with a note that for complex or first-time applicants, a pre-submission review from an [SBDC](https://upmetrics.co/blog/small-business-development-centers-sbdc) or a lender-side packager can catch issues before underwriting does.

Add an **Investor view** callout: investor diligence includes reference calls, customer calls, cohort-level data exports, and often a technical or legal review. Similar principle — plan starts it, diligence finishes it.

## 9. FAQ Section (Optional)

> 4 FAQs selected for natural reader follow-up. Each checked against the outline — none are answered in the main sections.

**Q1:** What is the minimum debt service coverage ratio lenders look for?
**A1:** Most SBA and conventional lenders want a DSCR of at least 1.25x; some want 1.50x for riskier industries. Answer in 2–3 sentences, cite that SBA guidance is published in SOP 50 10 8.
**Source:** Editorial judgment + Alloy Dev Co reference
**Why not in main content:** The DSCR section explains the calculation but not the threshold shopping across lenders.

**Q2:** How long should a business plan be for a lender?
**A2:** 20–30 pages for most SBA applications; shorter if the business is simple, longer if the deal is complex. Note that lenders read for clarity over length — a tight 18-page plan beats a padded 35-page one.
**Source:** Editorial judgment
**Why not in main content:** Tangential to the section-by-section framing.

**Q3:** Do I need a business plan for every type of business loan?
**A3:** Not every loan. Merchant cash advances, short-term online loans, and equipment financing under ~$50,000 often do not. SBA loans, conventional term loans, and loans above ~$100,000 almost always do.
**Source:** Editorial judgment + general lending practice
**Why not in main content:** Scope question — readers may ask before they even start writing.

**Q4:** How is a business plan for investors different from a business plan for lenders?
**A4:** Same core structure, different emphasis. Investors want growth, market size, and exit logic; lenders want cash flow, collateral, and repayment. Keep one plan, but pull different sections forward depending on the audience.
**Source:** Editorial judgment
**Why not in main content:** Summary-level question that ties together the "Investor view" callouts used throughout the article.
